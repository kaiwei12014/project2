              To avoid overfitting (and the need for early stopping at 80 trees), you can use stochastic gradient boosting (e.g. `subsample=0.5`). Beware that you will need to re-tune the optimal `learning_rate` parameter. See the tricks in Peter Prettenhofer's PyData presentations:

              https://www.google.fr/searc...

              Also to greatly improve the speed of the tuning the number of estimators, you can do that incremental with `warm_start=True`:

              gbr = GradientBoostingRegressor(warm_start=True, learning_rate=.05, max_depth=10, min_samples_leaf=20, random_state=0)
              for n_estimators in range(10, 501, 10):
              ____gbr.set_params(n_estimators=n_estimators)
              ____gbr.fit(X_train, y_train) # this will only fit 10 new trees and keep the previous trees in gbr
              ____# compute score on train and validation folds
              ____...

              (Sorry for the leading `____`, disqus does not preserve indenting).

              You might also be able to improve upon the raw GRBT predictions by fitting a final linear regression model on categorical (one hot) features extracted by the GBRT model (one binary feature for each leaf of each tree of the ensemble) in the spirit of what is done for classification by:

              "Practical Lessons from Predicting Clicks on Ads at Facebook"
              Junfeng Pan, He Xinran, Ou Jin, Tianbing XU, Bo Liu, Tao Xu, Yanxin Shi,
              Antoine Atallah, Ralf Herbrich, Stuart Bowers, Joaquin Quiñonero
              Candela
              International Workshop on Data Mining for Online Advertising (ADKDD)
